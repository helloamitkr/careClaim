01 — The problem

The transition-of-care coordination gap

In the United States, the days between hospital discharge and the next appointment are the single most dangerous stretch in a patient's journey — and the system responsible for shepherding them through it runs on phone tag, faxes, and overloaded care managers.

Where care breaks down

Roughly one in five Medicare patients is readmitted within 30 days of discharge, and a large share of those readmissions are considered preventable — driven not by disease progression but by coordination failure: unfilled prescriptions, referrals that never got booked, follow-ups the patient didn't know they had, prior authorizations stuck in a payer queue. CMS Conditions of Participation require hospitals to plan the discharge, but the plan often dies the moment the patient leaves the building.

The people responsible are drowning

Care managers — usually nurses or social workers — are the human glue holding transitions together. They spend the majority of their day on manual coordination work: calling SNFs to check bed availability, faxing referrals, chasing prior auths, leaving voicemails, re-entering the same patient data into three different systems. Every hour spent on this is an hour not spent on the patients who actually need clinical judgment.

The failures cluster in predictable, unglamorous ways

  • Prior authorization takes days when the patient needed home health tomorrow.
  • The SNF the discharge planner picked has no beds.
  • The patient's phone number is wrong, or they don't answer, or they don't speak English well enough to understand the voicemail.
  • The patient has no transportation to the follow-up appointment.
  • The patient has no PCP on file, so there's no one to follow up to.

Each of these is well-known. None of them are being handled proactively at scale.

Existing tools are the wrong shape

Care coordination platforms today are mostly tracking systems: dashboards that show a care manager what's broken, after it's already broken. They surface work; they don't do work. The gap isn't visibility — it's execution capacity.

Who is hurt

Patients — especially older adults, non-English speakers, patients without stable transportation or a PCP — experience preventable adverse events, ER revisits, and readmissions. Care managers burn out doing coordination work below the top of their license. Health systems and payers absorb readmission penalties, HEDIS misses, and referral leakage losses.

02 — The fix

How CareBridge AI fixes it

Transition coordination is a workflow problem, not a decision problem. The clinical decisions are already being made correctly at discharge. What's failing is the fifty small, boring, sequential actions that need to happen afterward.

That's exactly the shape of work a well-scoped multi-agent system can do — and exactly the shape of work where full autonomy is dangerous. Replace the manual coordination layer with specialized AI agents that execute the routine steps, escalate the ambiguous ones to a human, and log every action with a rationale and confidence score — so care managers spend their time on judgment, not on chasing referrals.

How the pieces map to the problem

Problem CareBridge response
Discharge breakdown Discharge Readiness Agent — reads the summary, checks med reconciliation, flags coherence issues (e.g. "home" disposition with no PCP on file).
Referral leakage & prior-auth delay Referral Routing Agent — selects in-network provider, checks payer prior-auth rules, submits and monitors. Delay pattern → escalation, not workaround.
Missed follow-ups Follow-Up Scheduling Agent — books the appointment, watches whether it actually happens. No-show is an event, not a dead end.
Patients falling out of reach Patient Outreach Agent — multi-channel protocol (phone → SMS → portal), respects language and preferred channel, stops after N attempts.
Compounding risk Risk & Escalation Agent — combines signals into a composite score, names the specific factors when it escalates.
"AI making clinical decisions" objection Human Review Gate Agent + audit log — no clinically-relevant action takes effect without human approval. Agents propose, humans dispose.
Care manager overload Clean transitions never hit the queue. The workbench shows decisions to make, not tasks to grind through.

What CareBridge AI is deliberately not

  • Not autonomous clinical decision-making. No medication changes, no risk reclassifications, no care-plan edits without a human in the loop.
  • Not a HIPAA-compliance claim. Designed with HIPAA-aligned guardrails; real certification is a deployment concern.
  • Not a replacement for care managers. A force multiplier for them.
  • Not a black box. Every agent decision is explainable via its logged rationale and confidence score.

03 — Data source

Where the data actually comes from

Discharge events don't appear from nowhere. They come from EHRs — Epic, Cerner, Meditech — via messages that are older, uglier, and more constrained than any REST API you've built before.

Every CareBridge transition case starts with a real event inside a hospital's EHR: a patient's encounter status flips to "discharged." The question is not whether data comes from the EHR, but which protocol we listen on and how we normalize it before anything else in the system sees it.

The four channels EHRs actually speak

Protocol Used for Reality check
HL7 v2 ADT Real-time admission, discharge, transfer events (ADT^A03 = discharge) The workhorse of hospital messaging. Pipe-delimited, 40+ years old, still the primary source of discharge events in most US hospitals.
FHIR R4 Encounter, Patient, MedicationRequest, Condition resources over REST/JSON The modern standard. Epic and Cerner both expose it. Adoption is uneven across smaller systems.
C-CDA Structured XML clinical documents — including full discharge summaries Where the narrative text lives. This is what the Discharge Readiness Agent actually reads.
SMART on FHIR Auth + launch context for apps embedded inside the EHR Relevant if the care manager workbench eventually launches from inside Epic (App Orchard) rather than as a standalone web app.

The EHR integration adapter — a new tier at the top

A dedicated layer sits above FastAPI whose only job is to translate incoming EHR messages into the internal Pydantic schema. Everything downstream of this layer is protocol-agnostic — agents don't know or care whether the source was Epic HL7, Cerner FHIR, or a synthetic feed.

Fig. 01 — EHR integration adapter with three interchangeable source parsers

Data lineage in the audit log

Every field in a normalized TransitionCase is tagged with its source — was it from the ADT MSH segment, a FHIR Encounter resource, a C-CDA discharge summary, or synthesized by an agent? "Where did this data come from" is as auditable as "who acted on it."

class TransitionCase(BaseModel):
    case_id: str
    patient_id: str
    admitting_facility: str
    discharge_date: date
    discharge_disposition: DischargeDisposition
    # Every clinical field carries its provenance:
    _source: DataSource         # ehr_hl7_v2 | ehr_fhir_r4 | ccda | synthetic
    _source_message_id: str     # the specific ADT or FHIR resource ID
    _received_at: datetime      # when the adapter ingested it

Hackathon vs. pilot

Phase What we build
Demo Synthetic feed only. A "New Discharge" button in the UI (or a Python script) drops a fixture JSON into the normalization layer. Same shape as production — just fed from a file instead of a hospital.
Pilot Add the HL7 v2 listener (usually a Mirth Connect channel or a Python hl7apy MLLP server) and the FHIR subscription. The normalization layer, FastAPI, and every agent stay unchanged.
Production Wrap each EHR connection as an MCP server. Multiple health systems, one interface, per-tenant auth and audit. Adding a third or fourth EHR becomes a config change, not a code change.
Why this matters for judges

Most hackathon healthcare demos handwave the data source with "assume Epic gives us this." Real judges — especially ex-clinicians and payer-side technologists — know that the EHR integration is the hard part. Naming the specific protocols (HL7 v2 ADT^A03, FHIR Encounter, C-CDA), the specific libraries, and the specific adapter pattern shows you know where real healthcare software actually gets stuck.

04 — Architecture

System architecture

Five tiers, top to bottom: the workbench users touch, the API that fronts everything, the orchestrator + event bus that routes work, the agents that do the work, and the persistence layer that makes it all replayable.

Fig. 02 — Five-tier architecture

The human review gate is not optional wiring — it's the architectural answer to "won't the AI make a bad clinical decision?" The answer is: it structurally can't, because the action doesn't take effect until the loop back to the workbench closes.

05 — Data flow

Data flow, top to bottom

What actually happens when a discharge event enters the system. Causal order, not clock time — Patient A's whole flow might take 2 hours, Patient C's might span 9 days.

Fig. 03 — Causal data flow from discharge to persistence

The amber diamond is where the "agents propose, humans dispose" rule actually lives. If composite confidence is above threshold and no risk factors tripped, the case auto-closes. If not, the flow is blocked at the Human Review Gate — nothing takes effect until a care manager acts. When they do, that decision re-enters the event bus as its own event, so downstream agents pick up the approved action, not the original proposal.

06 — Tech stack

What we're building with

The smallest stack that could realistically be extended into a production pilot. Every piece has a Postgres/Kafka/Temporal-shaped upgrade path — we didn't build those for a two-week demo.

Layer Tool Why
EHR ingress hl7apy · fhir.resources · Mirth Connect (pilot) Parses HL7 v2 ADT and FHIR Encounter resources; normalizes to internal schema before FastAPI sees them.
UI Next.js 14 · TypeScript · Tailwind · Zustand App Router for fast case list, typed API contracts, minimal state boilerplate.
API FastAPI · Pydantic v2 Async native, auto-generated OpenAPI docs, free request validation.
Orchestration Python asyncio · SQLite-backed event log In-process, replayable, no external broker for the demo.
Data SQLite → Postgres-portable schema Zero-setup demo; ANSI-standard SQL + CHECK constraints run unchanged on Postgres.
Agents Python classes; Claude API where language matters, deterministic Python elsewhere Right tool per agent — not LLM-everywhere.
Safety Audit log table · HITL gate · confidence thresholds Every action explainable and reversible.
Testing Pytest · pytest-asyncio 50-case suite covering happy path, escalation, and state recovery.

Deliberately not using

Kafka, Temporal, Celery, RabbitMQ. Over-engineering for a two-week build. The event bus abstraction means we can swap asyncio for Temporal later without changing agent code — which is the right answer.

07 — Agent anatomy

What is an agent, really?

An agent is a role in a system, not a technology. What sits inside — an LLM call, a Python rule engine, or both — is a design choice.

The "agent-ness" comes from the shape — input schema, output schema, confidence score, escalation threshold, audit log entry — not from whether an LLM is involved. This matters for judges: it means most of CareBridge is deterministic Python, and that's a feature.

How the eight agents split

Agent Type Why
Discharge Readiness LLM Parses narrative discharge summary text.
Referral Routing Python Rule-based payer + network + specialty lookup.
Follow-Up Scheduling Python Calendar math and slot availability.
Medication Instruction Hybrid DB lookup + LLM for plain-language rewrite.
Patient Outreach Hybrid Retry logic in Python, message generation via LLM.
Risk & Escalation Python Weighted numeric scoring across signals.
Human Review Gate Python Workflow state machine, blocks on human input.
Orchestrator Python Event routing, subscription management.

1 pure LLM, 5 pure Python, 2 hybrid. Using an LLM for rule-based lookup would add cost, latency, and non-determinism for no benefit.

What every agent shares

class Agent:
    input_schema:  PydanticModel   # typed input contract
    output_schema: PydanticModel   # typed output contract

    async def run(self, event) -> AgentOutput:
        result = self._decide(event)
        audit_log.write(
            agent_id=self.name,
            input_summary=event.summary(),
            confidence_score=result.confidence,
            decision=result.decision,
            rationale=result.rationale,
        )
        if result.confidence < self.threshold:
            return escalate_to_human(result)
        return result

Whether _decide() calls Claude or runs a lookup table, the shape is identical. The bus doesn't care what's inside the box.

The judge answer

"Two deployments for the demo. The architecture cleanly splits into four to five services for a pilot — Postgres replaces SQLite, agents move into a dedicated worker, Redis backs the event queue for crash recovery. We deliberately did not build that split for a two-week hackathon; the event bus abstraction means we can swap it in without changing agent code."

09 — What comes next

From analysis to shipping

  1. Phase 1 — Data model & mock data. Pydantic schemas, SQL DDL, three synthetic patients (A: clean, B: prior-auth delay, C: high-risk escalation).
  2. Phase 2 — Agent layer. Eight Python classes against the Phase 1 schema. Confidence scoring, escalation thresholds, unit tests using A/B/C.
  3. Phase 3 — Orchestration & event bus. Wire the agents into the asyncio + SQLite event log. Walk A/B/C through the full sequence.
  4. Phase 4 — API layer. FastAPI routes for case creation, agent action retrieval, human approve/override, audit log query.
  5. Phase 5 — UI. Care Manager Workbench, Transition Case View, Escalation Console, wired to the Phase 4 API.
  6. Phase 6 — Test suite & demo script. 50 test cases across the taxonomy; a 5-minute click-by-click narrative ending on the human-override moment.
